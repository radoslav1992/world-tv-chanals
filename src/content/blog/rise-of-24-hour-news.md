Today we take it for granted that news is available every hour of every day. But the idea of a television channel that does nothing but report the news, around the clock, was once considered a gamble that might never pay off. Here's how 24-hour news went from a risky experiment to a global institution.

## A bold experiment

When Ted Turner launched **CNN** — the Cable News Network — on 1 June 1980, the conventional wisdom was that audiences would never watch news for more than half an hour at a time. Critics nicknamed it the "Chicken Noodle Network." CNN proved them wrong. By committing to continuous coverage, it could break stories the moment they happened rather than waiting for a scheduled bulletin.

## The moment that changed everything

CNN's defining moment came with its live coverage of the 1991 Gulf War, when its correspondents reported from Baghdad as events unfolded. For the first time, a global audience watched a major conflict in real time. The "CNN effect" entered the language, and 24-hour news became an essential part of the media landscape.

## A wave of competitors

CNN's success inspired a wave of rivals. In the United Kingdom, **Sky News** launched in 1989, becoming Britain's first round-the-clock channel; the **BBC** followed with its own continuous news service in 1997. In the United States, **Fox News** and MSNBC arrived in 1996. Around the world, new entrants such as **Al Jazeera** (1996), **France 24** (2006) and **DW**'s television service brought fresh perspectives to global news.

## The multilingual, global era

Modern news channels are no longer tied to one country or language. Networks broadcast simultaneously in several languages, hand off live coverage between studios on different continents, and reach audiences directly through websites, apps and online video. **Euronews** broadcasts the same footage in many languages at once; **Al Jazeera English** "follows the sun" between broadcasting centres around the world.

## News on your terms

The latest shift is towards watching on demand and online. Most major news channels now stream live for free over the internet, which means you can compare how different broadcasters cover the same story — a British, French, German, American or Middle Eastern view, side by side.
