Alongside the commercial networks and streaming giants sits a different kind of television: public-service broadcasting. Funded by the public rather than purely by advertising, these broadcasters have a remit to inform, educate and entertain. Here's a look at how they work and why they still matter.

## What makes a broadcaster "public"?

A public-service broadcaster is owned by, or operates on behalf of, the public rather than private shareholders. Its mission is usually written into law or a charter, and emphasises impartial news, educational and cultural programming, and universal access — the idea that everyone, regardless of income or location, should be able to watch.

## How they're funded

Funding models vary:

- **Licence fee:** The BBC is funded mainly by a fee paid by UK households. This keeps its channels free of advertising.
- **Government budget:** Germany's international broadcaster DW is funded from the federal budget; Germany's domestic public broadcasters ARD and ZDF rely largely on a household broadcasting contribution.
- **Mixed funding:** America's PBS combines government support, member-station dues, corporate underwriting and viewer donations.

These models are designed to give broadcasters a degree of independence from both commercial pressure and direct political control.

## The major players

- **BBC (United Kingdom):** Often regarded as the model public broadcaster, with a global newsgathering operation and a vast output of drama, factual and entertainment programming.
- **PBS (United States):** America's public broadcaster, famed for documentaries, science, the arts and beloved children's programming.
- **ARD and ZDF (Germany):** Two large public broadcasters providing news, drama and regional programming across Germany.
- **Rai (Italy)** and **RTVE (Spain):** National public broadcasters whose flagship channels — Rai 1 and La 1 — remain central to cultural life and carry major national events.

## Why it still matters

In an age of fragmented streaming and algorithm-driven feeds, public broadcasters offer something distinctive: programming made in the public interest rather than purely for profit. Their news divisions are held to high standards of impartiality, their educational and cultural output fills gaps the market often ignores, and their content is typically free to watch.
